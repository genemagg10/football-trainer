For parents

The single biggest predictor of whether a kid keeps playing football is whether they enjoy it. The single biggest predictor of whether they enjoy it is the car ride home. We're going to spend most of this section on that car ride.

The 5:1 ratio

For every one piece of corrective feedback, give five pieces of specific praise. This isn't soft — it's how the brain consolidates skill. "I loved how you took that throw-in quickly" beats "you should've passed there" every time.

The car ride home

Three sentences only: "I loved watching you play." "Did you have fun?" "What do you want for dinner?" Save tactical analysis for when they ask. (They will, eventually. On their terms.)

Sideline behaviour

Cheer effort, not goals. Don't coach from the line — it splits the player's focus between you and their actual coach. Never criticize the referee in front of kids; they internalize disrespect for authority faster than tactics.

Multi-sport, please

Early specialization (one sport, year-round, before age 12) is associated with higher injury rates and earlier dropout. Encourage your kid to swim, run, climb, ski — anything that isn't football, for at least one season a year, until they're 14.

Sleep is training

Teen athletes need 9–10 hours of sleep. Performance drops measurably below 8. Phones out of bedrooms. Practice doesn't replace sleep — it depends on it.

Watch for burnout

Warning signs: stops practicing on their own, dreads sessions, performance plateau, irritability, frequent injuries. Sometimes the answer is rest. Sometimes it's a different team. Sometimes it's a different sport. Listen.

The Youth Football Trainer parent code

  1. I will let the coach coach.
  2. I will not yell instructions during games.
  3. I will respect the referee, even when they are wrong.
  4. I will praise effort, not just goals.
  5. I will not compare my child to other kids — out loud or in private.
  6. I will know that fewer than 1 in 1,000 youth players plays professionally — and love them either way.
  7. I will treat their teammates' parents like neighbors, even when we lose.
  8. I will trust that joy > outcomes, every single time.

For coaches

Coaching kids is one of the highest-leverage jobs in society. You will be remembered by kids who are now 30, 40, 50 years old. Don't waste it. Here's a starter checklist.

The 4-corner session plan

Every session should explicitly touch technical, tactical, physical, psychological/social. Most amateur sessions only hit technical + physical. The other two are where the real development lives.

Plan for 15+ touches per minute

If kids aren't averaging at least 15 touches per minute in the first half of practice, something is wrong with your design — usually too many lines, too few balls, or too much coach talk.

Talk less. Coach with questions.

"What did you see?" "What were your options?" "What would you try next time?" The kid who can answer those questions is becoming a footballer. The kid being told the answer is becoming a follower.

Bio-band when you can

If you have access to maturation data, group by it for some sessions. If not, at minimum: recognize that your 5'1" U13 and your 5'10" U13 need different challenges, even if the calendar disagrees.

Run the FIFA 11+

Or 11+ Kids, or PEP, or Knee Control. Pick one. Run it every session. Especially with girls. It's free, it's evidence-based, it cuts injury rates in half. Stop debating it.

Rotate everything

Through U12, every player plays every position — including goalkeeper. Through U14, captain rotates monthly. Through U16, set-piece taker rotates. Specialization is the destination, not the starting point.

Session plan template (60 min)

  1. 0–5 min: arrival activity — every kid with a ball, free play.
  2. 5–20 min: warm-up + ball mastery (FIFA 11+ Kids if applicable).
  3. 20–35 min: technical / 1v1 focus. Match today's theme.
  4. 35–50 min: small-sided game with a constraint that highlights the theme.
  5. 50–58 min: free-play scrimmage. Coach watches, does not coach.
  6. 58–60 min: one-question reflection: "What did you try today that worked?"

The 70% rule. If your players aren't moving with the ball at their feet for at least 70% of session time, redesign the session. Lines, lectures, and water breaks should be the other 30%, combined.

Safety & health

Long-term athletic development depends on short-term injury prevention. Below are the protocols that have the strongest evidence base and that we believe should be standard in any youth program.

Concussion & heading

  • US Soccer (since 2015): no deliberate heading at U10 and below. Limited heading U11–U13.
  • The FA (since 2020): no heading in training U7–U11. Maximum one session per week U12–U18 with low-pressure balls.
  • Suspected concussion: remove from play immediately. Return-to-play follows the SCAT-5 / CDC graded protocol. If in doubt, sit them out.

ACL prevention (especially for girls)

  • Female players are 3–6× more likely to suffer an ACL injury than males in the same sport.
  • The FIFA 11+ and 11+ Kids warm-ups reduce injury rates by 30–50% in published RCTs.
  • Run them every training session and as the pre-match warm-up. They take 15 minutes.
  • Focus on landing mechanics: knees over toes, soft hips, no knee valgus on landing.

Growth-plate & growth-spurt management

  • During Peak Height Velocity (PHV), reduce high-impact load and watch for Sever's, Osgood-Schlatter, and growth-plate injuries.
  • Coordination dips during the spurt — re-coach running mechanics, don't punish "regression."
  • Track height every quarter. A 6+ cm jump in a 6-month window = PHV. Treat that player accordingly.

RED-S & female athlete health

  • Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) impacts bone, hormones, and performance. Common in active teen girls.
  • Iron status is often low in menstruating athletes — annual screening is reasonable in elite environments.
  • Period absence (amenorrhea) is not a sign of fitness — it's a warning. See a sports physician.

Mental health

  • Burnout, performance anxiety, and disordered eating affect a meaningful minority of academy and competitive youth players.
  • Coaches and parents are not therapists — but you are screeners. Know the signs and have a referral pathway.
  • Normalize talking about pressure. The strongest pros (e.g. Aaron Ramsdale, Andrés Iniesta) have spoken openly about it.

Disclaimer. This page summarizes consensus from FIFA, the FA, US Soccer, the AAP, and BJSM. It is not medical advice. For an individual athlete, follow your team's medical staff or your child's pediatrician/sports physician.

Glossary

Football has its own dialect. Here are the terms used across this site, in plain English.

4-corner model
The FA's framework: technical, tactical, physical, psychological/social. Every session should touch all four.
Bio-banding
Grouping players by maturation stage (e.g. percentage of predicted adult height) rather than chronological age, to give early- and late-developers fair training environments.
FIFA 11+ / 11+ Kids
Evidence-based 15-minute warm-up programs designed to reduce non-contact injuries — especially ACL — by 30–50%.
Funino
The DFB's signature small-sided game format: 3v3 with two mini-goals at each end. Designed to maximize 1v1 actions and decision-making.
Gegenpressing
"Counter-pressing" — winning the ball back within ~5 seconds of losing it. Associated with Klopp / Rangnick / Tuchel.
Juego de posición
"Positional play." Spanish/Catalan philosophy of dividing the pitch into zones with rules about how players occupy them. Associated with Cruyff, Guardiola, La Masia.
LTAD
Long-Term Athletic Development. Framework that maps training emphasis (FUNdamentals → Train to Train → Train to Compete → Train to Win → Active for Life) to developmental stage.
PHV
Peak Height Velocity — the period of fastest growth during adolescence. Roughly 11–13 in girls, 13–15 in boys. The window of greatest injury risk and trainability change.
RAE (Relative Age Effect)
The bias in youth talent identification that favors players born early in the selection year (e.g. January–March in many federations). A meaningful problem in academy intakes.
RED-S
Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport. A syndrome from chronic underfueling that affects bone, hormones, and performance. Common in endurance and aesthetic-based sports; a real risk in football.
Rondo
Small-sided possession drill (e.g. 4v1, 5v2) in which outnumbered players keep the ball away from defenders. Daily staple at La Masia and most top-flight European clubs.
Small-sided game (SSG)
Game format with fewer than 11 per side (typically 3v3 to 7v7) on a smaller pitch. Maximizes touches, decisions, and 1v1s — the engine of youth development.
TIPS
Dutch player profile: Technique, Insight, Personality, Speed.
Transition
The moment the ball changes from one team to the other. The most chaotic and decisive seconds of any match.

About this project

Youth Football Trainer is a free, non-commercial educational resource. The goal is to translate the best of European youth-development thinking — most of which is fragmented across national languages, federation PDFs, and academic journals — into something a parent, coach, or curious player can actually use.

What this site is

  • An educational reference, not a curriculum to follow rigidly.
  • A starting point for parents and amateur coaches who don't have access to a UEFA license course.
  • A signpost to the original sources, which are always more authoritative than any summary.

What this site is not

  • Affiliated with any federation, club, or commercial coaching brand.
  • A substitute for in-person coach education.
  • Medical, nutritional, or psychological advice for an individual athlete.

Editorial principles

  1. Cite, translate, link. Every claim links back to a federation, coach, researcher, or peer-reviewed paper.
  2. Kid first, then player. When in doubt, choose the option that protects long-term love of the game.
  3. Boys and girls are different. We say so explicitly, with the science.
  4. Inclusion is the floor, not the ceiling. Late developers, neurodivergent players, disability football, and mixed-gender play all belong here.
  5. Show your work. If we're wrong, we want to know — corrections welcome.

Help make this better. If you're a federation coach educator, sports physician, or researcher and you spot something we should refine, please get in touch via the GitHub repository linked in the footer.
