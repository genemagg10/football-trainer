Developmental stages

From first touch to first contract.

Seven stages, mapped to the science of how kids grow and learn. Each stage covers what to teach, how to teach it, what's different for boys and girls, where the balance lives between individual mastery and team play, and which drills the top European academies use.

U6 U8 U10 U12 U14 U16 U18+

How to read this page. Ages are guidelines, not gates. We follow the LTAD (Long-Term Athletic Development) model — group by maturation when possible, not just birthday. Coaches: pick the stage that fits the player in front of you, not the calendar.

U6
Stage 1 · Discovery · ages 4–6

Fall in love with the ball.

There are no tactics. There is no team. There is a child, a ball, and joy. Everything else is a distraction at this age.

Physical

Gross motor skills, agility, balance, coordination (ABCs). Running, jumping, hopping, skipping.

Technical

Many touches per minute. Dribbling with both feet, simple stops, toe-taps.

Tactical

Almost none. The "tactical" lesson is: the ball goes in the goal.

Psychological

Joy, curiosity, imagination. Praise effort, never outcome.

Social

Parallel play. Sharing. Listening for 30 seconds at a time.

👦 Boys at this age

  • Slightly higher activity baseline; tend to engage with chase/race games.
  • Often more distracted in queues — keep activities flowing.
  • Same skill ceiling as girls. Treat them identically.

👧 Girls at this age

  • Often slightly ahead in fine motor coordination & verbal instructions.
  • Mixed sessions are ideal — gender separation isn't necessary or beneficial yet.
  • Storytelling and imaginative play land especially well.

🧍 Individual focus (90%)

  • Every kid has their own ball, every session.
  • Ratio of player-to-ball touches matters more than passes.
  • Free dribbling, "ball is your best friend" play.

👥 Team focus (10%)

  • Tag-style team games to introduce cooperation.
  • Maximum game format: 3v3, no goalkeeper.
  • No standings, no formal positions, no offside.

Coach principle. If a 5-year-old leaves practice tired, smiling, and wanting to bring the ball home, you've coached a perfect session. Everything else is bonus.

Featured drills from European academies

Sharks & minnows

Every player dribbles across a 15×15 yd box. 1–2 "sharks" without a ball try to kick balls out. If a shark gets your ball, you become a shark. Last minnow wins — for two seconds, then play again.

Dribbling 12+ touches/min

Source: The FA · England Skills Programme

Funino (3v3, four mini-goals)

The DFB's signature U6–U10 format. Two mini-goals per team forces decision-making (which goal is open?), constant scanning, and 4–5× more 1v1 actions than 7v7. No goalkeepers.

Decision-making 3v3

Source: DFB · Horst Wein methodology

Animal walks with the ball

"Sneak like a cat," "stomp like a dinosaur," "hop like a kangaroo" — each animal forces a different surface of the foot (sole, laces, inside, outside). Tom Byer's "ball mastery first" philosophy in action.

Ball mastery Imagination

Source: Tom Byer · "Soccer Starts at Home"

U8
Stage 2 · Foundation · ages 7–8

Build a personal relationship with the ball.

This is when ball mastery either takes root or it doesn't. Repetition is your best friend; standing in lines is your worst enemy.

Physical

Coordination still developing. Speed-of-feet drills. Avoid heavy strength work.

Technical

Dribbling at speed, both feet, all surfaces. Ball lifts. Receiving with sole.

Tactical

"Find space." Spread out vs. swarm. Score in our goal, defend the other.

Psychological

Confidence. Try hard things. Fail safely. Celebrate effort & creativity.

Social

Cooperation begins to feel useful. Take turns. Listen to a 60-second instruction.

👦 Boys at U8

  • Tend toward physical dueling — channel into 1v1 contests.
  • Watch ego: protect kids who lose 1v1s. Pair losers with new winners.

👧 Girls at U8

  • Cooperative formats often produce more touches than competitive ones.
  • Coach voice matters: encouraging, specific, genuine. Avoid the "honey" tone.
  • Mixed-gender play is still ideal at this age (and many U8 leagues default to it).

🧍 Individual focus (75%)

  • Every player, every session, gets time alone with their ball.
  • Ball mastery menu: La Liga / Coerver-style toe-taps, V-pulls, scissors, step-overs.
  • 1v1 contests against varying opponents.

👥 Team focus (25%)

  • Game format: 4v4 to 5v5, mini-goals or small goals, no GK.
  • One simple game-day theme per week (e.g. "spread out when we have the ball").
  • No fixed positions. Everyone plays everywhere.

Featured drills

Rondo 4v1

Spain's most famous training tool. Four players in a 6×6 yd circle pass and keep the ball; one defender in the middle tries to win it. Two-touch max. Build to 5v2, 6v2 with age. The single best drill ever invented for first-touch quality and scanning.

Possession First touch

Source: RFEF · La Masia tradition · used daily by FC Barcelona since the Cruyff era

1v1 to two goals

Pitch is 12×8 yd with a mini-goal at each end. Attacker can score in either. Forces defender to delay rather than dive in. 60-second rounds; rotate.

1v1 Confidence

Source: KNVB · Coerver Coaching · Wiel Coerver method

2v2 + neutral

2v2 in a tight grid with a "joker" who always plays for the team in possession. Effectively 3v2. Teaches finding the spare player, supporting angles, and quick combinations.

Combinations Support

Source: DFB-Akademie · "spielintelligenz" (game intelligence) modules

U10
Stage 3 · Skill-building · ages 9–10

The "golden age" of motor learning.

Neurologically, kids absorb new motor patterns faster between roughly 9 and 12 than at any other point in life. Whatever you teach now, sticks. Use the window — but use it wisely.

Physical

Speed-of-feet, coordination, multi-directional movement. Light plyometrics with bodyweight. No heavy lifting.

Technical

Refined dribbling at speed, accurate passing 5–15 yd, receiving across the body, basic finishing.

Tactical

Width & depth in possession. Pressing the ball-carrier. Defending as a pair (cover & balance).

Psychological

Persistence. Dealing with mistakes. Beginning to coach themselves.

Social

Captaincy can rotate weekly. Players begin to understand fairness & rules.

👦 Boys at U10

  • Pre-puberty: physical differences are minimal. Skill rules.
  • Boys may need help verbalizing what they tried and what they'd change.

👧 Girls at U10

  • Some girls begin pubertal changes; growth-related coordination loss is normal.
  • Begin FIFA 11+ Kids warm-up: cuts ACL injury risk significantly.
  • Many federations recommend separate girls' teams from this age — based on retention research, not physiology.

🧍 Individual focus (60%)

  • Daily ball mastery menu (5–10 minutes).
  • 1v1 attacking moves: scissors, step-over, La Croqueta, body feint, Cruyff turn.
  • Both-foot finishing — no "weak foot."

👥 Team focus (40%)

  • Game format: 5v5 → 7v7. Introduce a goalkeeper.
  • Width & depth: "stretch the pitch in possession, shrink it without."
  • Free rotation of all positions, including GK.

No heading practice yet. US Soccer prohibits deliberate heading before U12. The English FA's 2020 guidance limits heading to "a maximum of one session per week" with low-impact balls from U12 onward, and recommends avoiding it altogether before U12. Long-term brain health > throw-in routine.

Featured drills

Position rondo (4v1+1)

Four outside players sit on the corners of a 10×10 box; one neutral plays as a "pivot" in the middle. Defender must work to win it. Players can only pass to the neutral with their second touch — forces a scan before the first touch.

Scanning Pivot play

Source: La Masia / Joan Vilà position-play modules

3v3 to goals (transition focus)

3v3 on a 25×15 yd pitch with full goals and goalkeepers. Coach calls "switch!" every 90 seconds — one team rests, fresh team enters. Maximizes high-intensity moments & transitions.

Transitions Conditioning

Source: The FA · England DNA — "in possession, out of possession, transitions"

Both-feet finishing circuit

Cone slalom for 8 yd, then finish into a small goal. Round 1 right foot only, round 2 left foot only, round 3 first-time finish off a coach pass. Track misses per side; the goal is balance.

Finishing Both feet

Source: Coerver Coaching · "Pyramid of Player Development"

U12
Stage 4 · Game intelligence · ages 11–12

Speed of thought, not just speed of foot.

Technique becomes useful only when paired with awareness. This is where rondos become positional, where scanning becomes a habit, and where puberty starts widening the gap between players.

Physical

Pre-PHV in most boys, mid-PHV in many girls. Coordination dips during growth spurts — be patient.

Technical

First touch under pressure. Driven and disguised passing. Receiving on the half-turn.

Tactical

Positional play (zones), pressing triggers, defensive line, switching the point of attack.

Psychological

Self-image & comparison emerge. Resilience matters. Intrinsic motivation must be protected.

Social

Identity formation begins. Belonging to "the team" is powerful — and risky if used wrong.

👦 Boys at U12

  • Most haven't hit Peak Height Velocity (PHV) yet.
  • Watch for over-reliance on early-developers' size advantage.
  • Bio-band drills where possible: group by maturation, not age.

👧 Girls at U12

  • Many girls are mid-PHV; coordination & running mechanics need re-coaching.
  • Menarche often within ±18 months — track period & energy if athletes are open.
  • Body-image conversations happen even if you don't bring them up. Coaches set the tone.
  • Drop-out rates spike. Friendship-first sessions retain players.

🧍 Individual focus (50%)

  • Position-specific repetition (e.g. centre-backs receiving under pressure).
  • Receiving across body, on the half-turn.
  • Decision-making drills: scan-pass-scan-receive.

👥 Team focus (50%)

  • Game format: 9v9. Introduce structured 1-3-2-3 or 1-3-3-2.
  • Pressing as a unit (cover shadow, ball-side trigger).
  • Build-from-the-back patterns.

The Relative Age Effect bites here. Across European academies, players born January–March can outnumber October–December players 4-to-1 in U12 selections. The talent isn't biased — the calendar is. Strategies: bio-banding, futures fixtures, expanded squad sizes, and "second chance" trials.

Featured drills

Three-zone build-up game

30×20 yd pitch divided into three vertical zones. Defender, midfielder, attacker per zone per team (3v3). Ball must touch each zone before scoring. Teaches vertical progression, third-man runs, and patience.

Build-up Positional play

Source: La Masia / Pep Guardiola juego de posición

Switch rondo (4v2)

4v2 in a 10-yd circle. Standard rondo rules, with one twist: the player who passes must call out a number 1–4 before they pass. The receiver must say it back before their first touch. Forces the head-up scan that defines elite players.

Scanning Cognitive load

Source: Geir Jordet research · "scanning frequency & performance" · adapted by FA & DFB

Pressing trigger 3v3+GK

Defending team waits in a mid-block. The trigger is a backwards pass from the opponent — that's when everyone presses. Teaches the "press as a unit" reflex German clubs drill obsessively.

Pressing Out of possession

Source: DFB-Akademie · Ralf Rangnick / Jürgen Klopp gegenpressing tradition

U14
Stage 5 · Train to train · ages 13–14

Bodies change. The game changes too.

Peak Height Velocity (PHV) rearranges everything: coordination, balance, tactical reads. This is the highest injury-risk window in youth football. Train smart, not maximally.

Physical

PHV for most. Bodyweight strength, mobility, proper running mechanics. Re-establish coordination.

Technical

Position-specific technique. Crossing, defending crosses, set-piece routines.

Tactical

11v11 introduced. Formations, pressing triggers, transitions, defensive shape.

Psychological

Identity, autonomy, self-evaluation. Goal-setting becomes useful.

Social

Peer dynamics & leadership. The locker room teaches more than the field.

👦 Boys at U14

  • Most enter PHV between 13–15. Hamstring & growth-plate injuries spike.
  • Coordination "drops" during the spurt — it's normal, not regression.
  • Strength training: bodyweight only. No barbells under coaching review.

👧 Girls at U14

  • Most are post-PHV. Wider hip-knee angle (Q-angle) increases ACL risk.
  • FIFA 11+ or Knee Control program every session. Cuts ACL injury risk by 30–50%.
  • Iron status & energy availability matter — RED-S is a real risk for active teen girls.
  • Drop-out peaks here. Coach competence & team culture predict retention.

🧍 Individual focus (40%)

  • Position-specific technical work (e.g. CB heading prevention work, FB crossing).
  • Movement quality — landing mechanics, deceleration, change of direction.
  • 1v1 attacking and defending against quality opposition.

👥 Team focus (60%)

  • Game format: 11v11. Introduce a base formation (often 1-4-3-3 or 1-4-4-2).
  • Phases of play (build-up, progression, finishing) drilled separately.
  • Set pieces — both attacking & defending.

Injury prevention is non-negotiable. 70% of ACL injuries are non-contact. The FIFA 11+ Kids and PEP programs add 15 minutes to a session and reduce injuries by up to 50%. They aren't optional for good coaching — they are good coaching.

Featured drills

Phase of play (5+GK v 4)

Half-pitch. Defending team in 1-4-2 against attacking 1-3-2 with a goalkeeper. Attacker must build from GK, through midfield, into the final third. Score within 30 sec or restart. Mirrors England DNA "in possession" units.

Build-up 11v11 prep

FIFA 11+ Kids warm-up

15-minute warm-up: jogging with technique, single-leg balance, hop-and-hold landings, deceleration patterns. Built specifically to reduce ACL and ankle injuries in youth players.

Switch the point of attack

Pitch split in two. 3v3 on each side. Ball must be switched (long pass or carry) for a goal to count. Forces players to recognize when "the other side is open" — the defining concept of positional play.

U16
Stage 6 · Train to compete · ages 15–16

Position, periodization, and a real game model.

Players become specialists. Sessions become periodized. Coaches start asking: "what kind of football do we want to play, and what do players need to play it?"

Physical

Post-PHV. Strength training under qualified coaching. Aerobic base & speed.

Technical

Position-specific mastery. Receiving under heavy pressure. Set-piece specialism.

Tactical

Game model: principles in/out of possession, transitions. Opposition analysis introduced.

Psychological

Performance mindset. Pre-match routines. Handling pressure & selection.

Social

Leadership roles. Mentoring younger players. Identity beyond football.

👦 Boys at U16

  • Strength gains & aerobic capacity now respond strongly to training.
  • Watch overtraining: school + club + showcase = burnout fuel.
  • Gym work introduced under qualified S&C — quality > load.

👧 Girls at U16

  • Continue 11+ / Knee Control. ACL risk does not go away with age.
  • Period tracking helps with training planning & symptom management.
  • Strength training now reduces injury risk & improves performance — encourage it.

🧍 Individual focus (35%)

  • Position role-clarity (e.g. inverted full-back, double pivot, false 9).
  • Individual S&C plan based on growth status.
  • Video review — players self-clip their best/worst moments.

👥 Team focus (65%)

  • Define the team's game model: 6–8 principles in/out/transition.
  • Periodized weekly schedule (recovery, acquisition, match-day).
  • Set-piece routines (≈ 30% of goals come from set pieces).

Featured drills

9-zone positional game

Pitch divided into 3 horizontal lines × 3 vertical channels. Rules limit how many players can occupy each zone. Forces width, depth, and movement to "free zones" — Pep Guardiola's blueprint for positional play.

Positional play Spacing

Source: Juego de Posición · Pep Guardiola / Juanma Lillo

Counter-press 5-second rule

Small-sided 6v6 to two goals. Whenever you lose the ball, you have 5 seconds to win it back before retreating into shape. Klopp's defining principle, drilled until it's reflex.

Gegenpressing Transition

Source: Liverpool FC / Borussia Dortmund · Jürgen Klopp game model

Set-piece library (corners)

Six rehearsed corner routines: near-post flick, far-post overload, edge-of-box drop, decoy run, short corner, "warzone" zonal mix. Repeated weekly. The single highest-ROI training time at U16+.

U18+
Stage 7 · Train to win · ages 17–18+

The bridge to senior football.

High performance: lifestyle, periodization, professionalism. The goal is no longer to develop — it's to perform while still developing. And to plan what happens if football doesn't work out.

Physical

Senior-level S&C, sprint mechanics, individual recovery protocols, sleep monitoring.

Technical

Position mastery under elite pressure. Two-footed competence at speed.

Tactical

Match analysis, opponent scouting, in-game adjustments, multi-formation literacy.

Psychological

Pressure handling, identity beyond football, dealing with rejection & selection.

Social

Professionalism, media literacy, social-media judgement, mentoring younger players.

👨 Young men at U18+

  • Senior-style strength training viable. Sprint & aerobic capacity peak years away.
  • Manage school + club + national-team load. Sleep is a non-negotiable training input.

👩 Young women at U18+

  • Strength & speed gains accelerate with proper training — they don't plateau.
  • RED-S, iron, bone density: monitor under medical advice.
  • Dual-career planning is critical given current pro pathway realities.

🧍 Individual focus (40%)

  • Individual development plan (IDP) per player, reviewed quarterly.
  • Position-specific scenario training with video.
  • Life skills: nutrition, sleep, finance, agents, social media.

👥 Team focus (60%)

  • Senior tactical literacy — multiple formations, in-game pivots.
  • Opposition analysis & pre-match plans.
  • Leadership council, captaincy rotation, locker-room standards.

Dual-career matters. Of all academy players in elite European systems, fewer than 1 in 200 plays a senior professional minute in a top-five league. A player's education, mental health, and identity outside football aren't afterthoughts — they're part of "professionalism."

Featured drills

Tactical periodization session

Vítor Frade's framework. Each week assigns one principle (e.g. "pressing trap on the strong side") to each day, scaled by intensity: acquisition Tuesday, principle Wednesday, sub-principle Thursday, activation Friday. Match-led, not fitness-led.

Periodization Game model

Source: Vítor Frade · University of Porto · adopted by FC Porto, Mourinho, Villas-Boas

Sprint & repeat sprint protocol

10×30m max sprints with full recovery (acceleration). Then 6×40m with 30s recovery (repeated sprint ability). Modeled on senior pro pre-season standards. Pair with hamstring eccentric work.

Speed RSA

Source: BJSM consensus · Nordic-board hamstring protocols

Self-analysis video routine

Each player clips 5 best and 5 worst moments from match footage and writes one sentence on each. Develops self-coaching, the marker that separates elite players from talented ones.
